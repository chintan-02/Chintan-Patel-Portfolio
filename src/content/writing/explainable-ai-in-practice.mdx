## It's Not One Technique

"Explainable AI" usually gets talked about as if it's a single tool you bolt onto a model — add SHAP, ship it, done. After building three systems that each needed explainability for a genuinely different reason, I don't think that's accurate. Each one needed something different, because each one was solving a different version of "why should anyone trust this output."

## TriageAI: Explaining a Score That Has Real Consequences

For a model predicting emergency severity, the question isn't just "why did the model say ESI level 3" — it's "should a clinician trust this enough to act on it, and what happens when the model is wrong." That's why TriageAI isn't just a model with SHAP values attached to it; it's a model plus clinical safety rules that can escalate the output, plus a clinician review step that can override it. SHAP explains *why* the model reached a number. The safety rules and human review exist because explaining a wrong answer doesn't make it a safe one — they're the part of the system that keeps the model's output from being treated as final clinical authority.

## ResumeIQ: Explaining Without a Single Score to Hide Behind

ResumeIQ's problem is the opposite shape. There's no single high-stakes prediction to explain — there's a resume, and a job seeker who wants to know *specifically* what to fix. A single opaque "match score" would actually be a worse product here, not just a less explainable one, because it gives someone a number with no actionable next step. So ResumeIQ doesn't really have one black-box score at all — it surfaces ATS-style signals, JD keyword overlap, matched and missing skills, and sentence-quality signals separately, as transparent components rather than one fused number. The explainability requirement shaped the product decision, not just the model output.

## PolicyGPT: Explaining by Refusing to Answer

A RAG assistant answering questions about HR and compliance documents has a different trust problem again — it's not "explain this score," it's "prove this answer is actually grounded in the source document, and tell me clearly when it isn't." That's what the evidence gate and page-level citations are for: every supported answer is traceable to a specific page, and when retrieval doesn't clear the confidence bar, the system says so explicitly instead of generating a plausible-sounding guess. Here, the most important explainability feature isn't a justification attached to an answer — it's the system's honesty about the edge of its own knowledge.

## The Common Thread

Underneath three different implementations, the actual requirement was the same each time: don't make someone take the system's word for it. Show the reasoning where a clinician needs to weigh it, decompose the score where a job seeker needs to act on it, and cite the source — or admit you can't — where the cost of being confidently wrong is high. That's the practical version of "intelligence you can trace": not a single library you import, but a design constraint that changes what the system actually outputs, project to project.

## What Explainability Doesn't Guarantee

It's worth being honest about the limit here. SHAP values explain what the model's decision boundary did, not whether the model's training data was representative or fair — those are separate problems that explainability alone doesn't solve. Transparent component scores in ResumeIQ make the *signals* visible, not necessarily complete. And an evidence-gated RAG system can still cite a source document that's outdated or wrong. Explainability tells you how a system reached its output. It doesn't, by itself, guarantee the output is correct — and treating it as if it does would be exactly the kind of false confidence these systems are supposed to avoid.
